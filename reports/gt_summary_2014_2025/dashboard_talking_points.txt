Talking Points for the Dashboard (GT 2014–2025 + Model vs Market)

How to frame it (one sentence)
- “This dashboard summarizes Georgia Tech’s 2014–2025 regular-season performance with uncertainty (confidence intervals), and includes a small 2025 sample comparing my model’s pregame win probabilities to market-implied probabilities.”

1) Record + Win-Rate Confidence Interval
What it shows:
- GT is 69–73 over 142 games (48.6% win rate).
- The 95% Wilson confidence interval is 40.5%–56.7%.

Why it’s impactful:
- The CI is the key: it tells you how uncertain the win rate estimate is from only 142 games.
- This keeps the conversation honest: you should describe this span as “around .500” rather than claiming a precise “48.6% team.”

How to explain it out loud:
- “The win rate is about 49%, but statistically the data supports something more like 41% to 57% as a plausible long-run range.”

2) Average Margin + Bootstrap Confidence Interval
What it shows:
- Mean margin is −0.58 points/game (GT slightly negative on average).
- The 95% bootstrap CI is −3.99 to +3.01, which crosses 0.

Why it’s impactful:
- Margin captures “how close” games are; it’s often more informative than record alone.
- The CI crossing 0 means you can’t confidently say GT was meaningfully positive or negative on average across the whole era.

How to explain it out loud:
- “On average GT was basically break-even in scoring margin over this span, and the uncertainty range includes both slightly better and slightly worse than even.”

3) Home/Away/Neutral Splits
What it shows:
- Home (n=73): 57.5% win rate (95% CI: 46.1%–68.2%).
- Away (n=60): 40.0% win rate (95% CI: 28.6%–52.6%).
- Neutral (n=9): 33.3% win rate (95% CI: 12.1%–64.6%).
- Home − Away difference: the 95% bootstrap CI is −17.2pp to +16.9pp (includes 0).

Why it’s impactful:
- The point estimate suggests home-field advantage, but the “home-minus-away” CI including 0 means the sample is noisy enough that you can’t claim it’s statistically definitive at 95%.
- Neutral-site is very small sample, so it’s mostly “illustrative,” not a strong conclusion.

How to explain it out loud:
- “The data suggests GT wins more at home than away, but given the uncertainty we should treat it as suggestive rather than proven from this sample alone.”

4) Win Rate by Year (Chart + Table)
What it shows:
- There is large season-to-season variation. Each season is ~12 games, so year-to-year differences can be partly random.
- In this dataset, 2014 and 2025 are strong seasons by win rate; several seasons from 2019–2022 are weaker.

Why it’s impactful:
- It shows the “non-stationarity” problem: teams change over time (coaches, players, scheme), so treating the entire 2014–2025 period as one constant level is misleading.
- The CI band emphasizes that some “peaks/valleys” are clearer than others.

How to explain it out loud:
- “This isn’t a flat process—GT has clear swings by season, and the error bars remind you that 12 games is a small sample.”

5) Margin Visuals (Histogram + by Year)
What it shows:
- The histogram explains whether the era is mostly close games or lots of blowouts.
- Margin by year helps identify ‘better-than-record’ or ‘worse-than-record’ seasons (margin can be more stable than one-score outcomes).

Why it’s impactful:
- It prevents overfocusing on only W/L, which can be heavily influenced by a few close outcomes.

How to explain it out loud:
- “Margin shows whether a season’s record was supported by consistent performance or by a handful of close-game breaks.”

6) 2025: Model vs Betting Market (Implied p-win) Table
What it shows (in your embedded table):
- Δ (model − market) highlights where you disagreed with the market.
- On the 7 rows that include results (W/L):
  - Model pick accuracy: 71% (5/7)
  - Market pick accuracy: 57% (4/7)
  - Brier score: model 0.210 vs market 0.236
  - Mean Δ: +4.2 percentage points; mean |Δ|: 6.1 points

How accurate is it?
- Call it “interesting but not conclusive.” This is a *tiny* sample and it’s not the full season table.
- Markets are strong benchmarks; beating them consistently is hard and requires larger samples to claim anything.

Why it’s impactful:
- It provides an external reference point and a good narrative: “Here’s where my model took a stand vs consensus.”
- It helps diagnose bias: e.g., if Δ is consistently positive/negative, the model may be systematically optimistic/pessimistic.

How to explain it out loud (responsible version):
- “In this small sample, my model slightly outperformed the market on accuracy/Brier, but it’s not enough data to claim I beat the market—this is more of a calibration and bias check.”
